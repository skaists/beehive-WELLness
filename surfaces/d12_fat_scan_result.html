Where the numbers came from

Every value carries its source and its basis. Basis is which food, measured against what — and it is the difference between two numbers disagreeing and two numbers describing different things.

1 determination one laboratory measured it
2 independent determinations two laboratories measured it separately
not measured nobody measured it — shown as itself, never as zero, never estimated from a similar food

A count is not a grade. Twelve national tables printing the same number is still 1 determination, because most of them copied it — the count follows each value back to the laboratory that actually measured it, so agreement between copies can't inflate it. And a high count is deliberately not coloured as success anywhere on this page: more measurements is more measurement, not proof. You draw the conclusion; this page just tells you what was counted.

Where sources disagree — including ours

This scanner shows disagreement instead of hiding it behind an average. All three of these are live, unresolved, and shown on purpose.

Hemp protein — 42% vs 31.6 g

Not a disagreement. These describe two different foods, so they are never compared and never averaged here.

A concentrate is a processed fraction; hulled hearts are the seed. Both numbers are correct about the thing they measured. Collapsing them into one row is how a scanner starts lying politely.

You cannot have 252 g of anything inside 100 g of food, so something is wrong — most likely a milligram figure sitting in a grams column. This page doesn't guess which, because it doesn't know whether the amount, the unit or the column slipped. Silently rewriting it to 252 mg would be inventing a measurement nobody took.

Two things the label could never tell you

This "saturated fat" has zero MCTs. It's all long-chain — so it behaves nothing like coconut's medium-chain saturated fat, even though the label prints the same word for both. And its omega-3 is all ALA — no EPA or DHA (those aren't in plants). Your body converts ALA→EPA→DHA only slightly, so "has omega-3" and "has EPA/DHA" are different claims. The label collapses both distinctions; the composition reveals them.
