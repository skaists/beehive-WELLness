Three things the panel is telling you

1 · Per 100 g is not per serving

Every number above is per 100 g. A common serving is ~30 g (about 3 tbsp). To get a serving, multiply by 0.30: protein becomes 31.6 × 0.30 ≈ 9.5 g, energy 553 × 0.30 ≈ 166 kcal. Panels quote per-100 g so foods compare fairly — you do the serving math.

2 · "Complete protein" is a real, checkable fact

Hemp hearts contain all nine essential amino acids in usable amounts — that's what "complete" means, and it's verifiable in the amino-acid breakdown, not a marketing word. ~31.6 g/100 g of complete plant protein is genuinely notable. (What that does for a given body is a different question, for a later module and honest evidence — not this panel.)

3 · The fat is mostly polyunsaturated

Of 48.8 g fat, 38.1 g is polyunsaturated — largely linoleic acid (omega-6) and alpha-linolenic acid (omega-3), in a ratio near 3:1. That the ratio exists is fact; whether a 3:1 ratio matters for you is a claim to weigh later, not read off the label.

The skill that outlasts this lesson: know your form

Same plant, three very different numbers

"Hemp protein" on two packages can mean two different foods. Protein per 100 g depends entirely on the form:

Hearts (hulled seed) ≈ 31.6 g Protein powder (concentrate) ≈ 42–50 g Whole seed (with hull) ≈ 25 g + more fiber

So if a comparison table lists hemp at "42%," it's almost certainly the powder, not the hearts. Neither is wrong — they're different foods. Reading well means asking which form before you trust the number.

And know your database

Lab-verified national databases are the trustworthy sources: USDA FoodData Central (US) and Health Canada's Canadian Nutrient File (CNF). A product's own back-of-pack panel may round differently or use a different lab. When numbers disagree, the question isn't "who's lying" — it's "different form, different lot, or different database?"
