The fats the label won't show you

Module 1 taught you to read the panel. But the two most important things about fat, the panel doesn't print: that "saturated" is three different molecules, and that not all omega-3 is the same omega-3.

1 · "Saturated" is not one thing — chain length is everything

All saturated fats share a chemistry, but their chain length changes how your body handles them. The label prints one word for all three:

C2–C6

Short-chain (SCFA)

Butyric, propionic, acetic. You mostly grow these — gut microbes ferment fiber into them; only traces in food (butter). Butyrate is the main fuel of your colon lining. (established)

C6–C12

Medium-chain (MCFA / MCTs)

Caprylic, capric, lauric. Coconut, MCT oil. Absorbed straight to the liver via the portal vein, largely skipping the bile/chylomicron route, and rapidly burned — even ketogenic. Metabolically distinct from all other saturated fat. (established)

C14–C18

Long-chain (LCFA)

Palmitic, stearic, myristic. Most saturated fat in most foods; needs chylomicron transport. Stearic behaves more neutrally than palmitic. (established, nuanced)

So coconut's saturated fat (mostly MCTs) and beef's (mostly LCFAs) are handled by different pathways — yet the label calls both just "saturated." That's the comprehension the label costs you.

2 · Two fats you must eat — the essentials

Your body builds most fats itself. Two it cannot — you must eat them, which is what "essential" literally means:

Omega-6 family — parent: linoleic acid (LA)
LA → GLA → DGLA → arachidonic acid (AA)
Omega-3 family — parent: alpha-linolenic acid (ALA)
ALA → EPA → DHA

LA and ALA are the two true dietary essentials. Run low on them long enough and you get a real, measurable deficiency (scaly skin, poor healing; biochemically, the "Mead acid" ratio rises) — documented in fat-free medical feeding. That deficiency is fact, not wellness folklore.

3 · "Has omega-3" ≠ "has EPA/DHA"

This is the single most-missed distinction, and the scanner makes it visible. Plant omega-3 is ALA (flax, chia, hemp, walnut). Marine omega-3 is EPA/DHA (fish, algae). Your body converts ALA → EPA → DHA only slightly — low single-digit percent to DHA, less in men. So a food labeled "rich in omega-3" may give you ALA and almost no EPA/DHA. Neither is bad; they are just not interchangeable, and the label blurs them into one word.

What's proved, and what's still argued

Proved

  • LA & ALA are essential; deficiency is real and measurable
  • MCTs are absorbed & metabolized differently (portal, ketogenic)
  • ALA→EPA→DHA conversion is inefficient in humans
  • Butyrate fuels colon cells
  • The chain-length and omega pathways themselves
  • WHO/FAO population nutrient goals (TRS 916 §5.1) — total fat 15–30%, saturated <10%, PUFA 6–10% (n-6 5–8%, n-3 1–2%), trans <1%, free sugars <10%, cholesterol <300 mg/d, salt <5 g/d (sodium <2 g/d), fruit & veg ≥400 g/d. Population goals from a named consultation — not individual targets.

Still argued — teach as claims

  • The "ideal" omega-6:omega-3 ratio and its disease effects
  • Saturated fat and heart-disease risk (contested; chain-dependent)
  • Chronic-disease outcomes of long-term low omega-3
  • MCT/ketone benefits beyond metabolism
