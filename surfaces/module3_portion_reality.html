Portion reality

A number on a panel is only half a fact. The other half is how much, for whom, in whose day. This module teaches you to size any claim against your own life — not to shrink it.

Sizing a claim against your day

Energy density is the lever

Hemp hearts are ~5.5 kcal/g — energy-dense, because they're ~49% fat. That's not good or bad; it's a fact you size. 100 g is ~553 kcal — a real chunk of a day for one food. 30 g is a topping. The skill is holding the density in view when you decide the amount, instead of reading "healthy" and forgetting the gram count.

bLOVErAi's job here — and its ceiling

Your interpreter can show you the math for your logged day and your context, in your language. What it will never do is hand you a target number, a deficit, or a "you should eat X." It sizes the question; you answer it. For anything clinical, its ceiling is one sentence: consider discussing this with a clinician.

The reframe that makes this healthy

Most nutrition apps ask "how do I eat less." We ask the question underneath: "am I getting my essentials at all?" Completeness, not restriction. You size portions up toward what's missing (the essential fats from Module 2, complete protein, minerals), not down toward a smaller number.

Sourced · WHO/FAO TRS 916

Energy-dense, micronutrient-poor foods are the pattern this module is about — WHO/FAO grades their high intake as convincing evidence for weight gain (TRS 916, Table 7), and notes that in many countries in economic transition, obesity and chronic undernutrition now coexist in the same population, sometimes the same household. Population-level, not a statement about you.

A note we mean: this course sets no goal weight, no calorie deficit, and no target you must hit — by design, those don't exist here. If tracking food ever starts to feel compulsive or distressing, that's worth pausing for. Support is real and available — in the US, the National Alliance for Eating Disorders helpline. You're a person learning to read, not a number to shrink.
